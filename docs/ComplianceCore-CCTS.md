## 1. Plain English Explanation — What Is CCTS?

India has created its own mandatory carbon market called the **Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS)**. It works like this:

1. The government picks the 9 most energy-intensive industrial sectors
2. Every large factory in those sectors gets an individual **GHG Emission Intensity (GEI) target** — a limit on how much CO2-equivalent they can emit per unit of product they make
3. If a factory does **better than** its target (emits less), it earns **Carbon Credit Certificates (CCCs)** which it can **sell** on stock exchanges for money
4. If a factory does **worse than** its target (emits more), it must **buy CCCs** from the market to make up the difference
5. If it neither buys nor fixes the shortfall, it pays a **penalty = 2× the market price × the tonnes of deficit**

This is India's equivalent of the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS). The first compliance period is FY 2025-26 (April 2025 to March 2026), and the first trading window opens in **October 2026**.

---

## 2. Why This Law Exists

India has committed to the Paris Agreement and its Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs):

- Reduce the emissions intensity of GDP by 45% below 2005 levels by 2030
- Achieve 50% of cumulative installed electric power capacity from non-fossil fuel sources by 2030

Heavy industry accounts for a large share of India's GHG emissions. The CCTS is designed to make industry reduce those emissions by creating a financial incentive — reducing emissions earns you money, exceeding limits costs you money.

The legal foundation is the **Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act 2022**, which added Part X to the original Energy Conservation Act 2001, giving the Central Government power to create and operate a carbon credit trading scheme.

## 4. What Is GEI — Greenhouse Gas Emission Intensity?

**GEI = Total GHG Emissions ÷ Total Production Output**

- **Units:** tCO2e per tonne of product (or per MBBLS of crude throughput for petroleum refineries)
- **GHG included:** CO2 primarily; also PFCs (CF4, C2F6, C4F10, C6F14) for aluminium smelters
- **GWP factors:** Applied per IPCC Assessment Reports
- **Scope 1:** Direct emissions — fuel combustion in boilers/furnaces + industrial process emissions (e.g., CO2 from lime calcination in cement)
- **Scope 2:** Indirect emissions from **purchased electricity and heat** — calculated using CEA grid emission factors for your state/region

### Example (Iron & Steel Plant)
A steel plant produced 1,000,000 tonnes of crude steel with:
- Scope 1 emissions: 2,200,000 tCO2e (coking coal + iron ore reduction)
- Scope 2 emissions: 300,000 tCO2e (purchased grid electricity)
- Total GHG: 2,500,000 tCO2e

**GEI = 2,500,000 ÷ 1,000,000 = 2.50 tCO2e per tonne crude steel**

## 7. How the Calculation Works — Step by Step

### Step A: Calculate Actual GEI

```
Actual GEI = (Scope 1 Emissions + Scope 2 Emissions) ÷ Annual Production

Example:
  Scope 1 = 2,200,000 tCO2e
  Scope 2 = 300,000 tCO2e
  Production = 1,000,000 tonnes crude steel

  GEI = (2,200,000 + 300,000) ÷ 1,000,000 = 2.50 tCO2e/tonne
```

### Step B: Compare to BEE-Notified Target

```
GEI Gap = Target GEI − Actual GEI

Positive gap → You are BELOW target → SURPLUS
Negative gap → You are ABOVE target → DEFICIT
```

### Step C: Calculate CCC Surplus or Deficit

```
CCCs = GEI Gap × Annual Production

Surplus Example (target met):
  Target GEI = 2.40, Actual GEI = 2.30 (better than target)
  GEI Gap = 2.40 − 2.30 = +0.10 tCO2e/tonne
  CCCs Earned = 0.10 × 1,000,000 = 100,000 CCCs

Deficit Example (target missed):
  Target GEI = 2.40, Actual GEI = 2.55 (worse than target)
  GEI Gap = 2.40 − 2.55 = −0.15 tCO2e/tonne
  CCCs Deficit = 0.15 × 1,000,000 = 150,000 CCCs to purchase
```

**1 CCC = 1 tCO2e**

### Step D: Calculate Estimated Revenue (Surplus) or Cost (Deficit)

```
Market price range (analyst estimate, no official band yet): ₹600–₹1,200/tCO2e

Surplus value = CCCs Earned × Market Price
  At ₹800/tCO2e (mid): 100,000 × ₹800 = ₹8,00,00,000 (₹8 Cr)

Deficit cost = CCCs Deficit × Market Price
  At ₹800/tCO2e (mid): 150,000 × ₹800 = ₹12,00,00,000 (₹12 Cr)
```

### Step E: Environmental Compensation (Penalty) if Deficit Uncorrected

```
Penalty = 2 × Market Price × CCC Deficit

At ₹800/tCO2e:  2 × ₹800 × 150,000 = ₹24,00,00,000 (₹24 Cr)
At ₹1,200/tCO2e: 2 × ₹1,200 × 150,000 = ₹36,00,00,000 (₹36 Cr)

NOTE: This penalty applies ONLY if the deficit is NOT corrected by purchasing
CCCs during the October 2026 trading window. It is not a fixed penalty —
it floats with the actual traded market price.
```

## 8. Complete Worked Example

**Plant:** RCF Trombay Fertiliser Complex, Maharashtra
**FY:** 2025-26

**Given data:**
- Annual urea production: 8,00,000 tonnes
- Scope 1 emissions: 2,08,00,000 tCO2e (gas combustion + process CO2)
- Scope 2 emissions: 8,00,000 tCO2e (purchased electricity)
- BEE-notified baseline GEI (FY 2023-24): 2.70 tCO2e/tonne urea
- BEE-notified target GEI for FY 2025-26: 2.645 tCO2e/tonne urea

**Calculation:**

```
Step A: Total GHG = 2,08,00,000 + 8,00,000 = 2,16,00,000 tCO2e

Step B: Actual GEI = 2,16,00,000 ÷ 8,00,000 = 2.70 tCO2e/tonne urea

Step C: GEI Gap = 2.645 − 2.70 = −0.055 (DEFICIT — actual exceeds target)

Step D: CCCs Deficit = 0.055 × 8,00,000 = 44,000 CCCs to purchase

Step E: If uncorrected:
  Penalty (at ₹800 mid) = 2 × ₹800 × 44,000 = ₹7,04,00,000 (₹7.04 Cr)
  Penalty (at ₹1,200)    = 2 × ₹1,200 × 44,000 = ₹10,56,00,000 (₹10.56 Cr)

Purchase cost on market (to avoid penalty):
  At ₹800 mid: 44,000 × ₹800 = ₹3,52,00,000 (₹3.52 Cr)

→ Cheaper to buy CCCs (₹3.52 Cr) than pay the penalty (₹7.04 Cr minimum).
```

**Result:** RCF Trombay must purchase 44,000 CCCs during the October 2026 trading window.

## 9. CCC Mechanics — How Carbon Credits Work Under CCTS

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| What is 1 CCC | 1 Carbon Credit Certificate = 1 tCO2e of emission reduction relative to target |
| Trading venues | IEX (Indian Energy Exchange), PXIL (Power Exchange India Ltd.), HPX |
| Settlement | T+1 (next-day settlement) |
| Banking | Unlimited — surplus CCCs may be saved and used in future compliance years |
| Borrowing | Not permitted — you cannot use future-year CCCs for current year compliance |
| Offsets | Not permitted under CCTS compliance mechanism (CCTS is separate from CCTS Offset Mechanism) |
| Who issues | NSC-ICM (National Steering Committee for Indian Carbon Market) on BEE recommendation |
| Trading suspension | 3+ defaults in a quarter → 6-month suspension from trading |
| Price band | Not yet published by CERC as of May 2026 (market-determined) |

## 10. The MRV Process — Monitoring, Reporting, Verification

MRV is the three-part process each obligated entity must follow every year:

### Monitoring (M)
- Set up a GHG Monitoring Plan covering all Scope 1 and Scope 2 emission sources
- Use CEA-specified grid emission factors for electricity (state-wise)
- Apply IPCC GWP factors for non-CO2 gases (relevant for aluminium PFCs)
- Record fuel consumption, process data, and electricity purchase monthly

### Reporting (R)
- Compile annual GHG data after FY ends (March 31)
- Fill **Form A** — BEE's official GHG emission reporting format
- Form A includes: entity details, production volume, fuel consumption by type, process emissions, electricity purchased, total Scope 1, total Scope 2, calculated GEI

### Verification (V)
- Hire a **BEE-accredited Carbon Verification Agency (ACVA)** — mandatory before submitting
- ACVA independently audits your emission data and production records
- ACVA issues a Verification Statement confirming the accuracy of your Form A
- Submit verified Form A to BEE by **July 31** each year

### After Submission
1. BEE checks completeness (10 working days)
2. BEE conducts technical review (30+ days)
3. NSC-ICM makes CCC issuance/surrender recommendation (2 weeks)
4. CCCs issued to surplus entities / surrender demand sent to deficit entities (2 weeks)
5. Trading window opens on IEX/PXIL/HPX (~October)
6. Deficit entities purchase CCCs; surplus entities sell
7. Environmental Compensation collected by CPCB from those who remain in deficit after trading window (90-day payment window)

## 12. Penalty Structure

**Environmental Compensation (EC):**

```
EC = 2 × Average CCC Traded Price × CCC Deficit (tCO2e)
```

- No fixed rupee amount — the penalty floats with the market price
- Administered by: Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB)
- Payment deadline: 90 days from CPCB order
- No criminal liability under CCTS for non-compliance — EC is the sole enforcement instrument
- EC funds held in a separate CPCB account; used on NSC-ICM recommendation for CCTS scheme purposes

**At different price scenarios:**

| Market Price | Penalty per tCO2e deficit |
|---|---|
| ₹600/tCO2e | ₹1,200/tCO2e |
| ₹800/tCO2e (mid-estimate) | ₹1,600/tCO2e |
| ₹1,200/tCO2e | ₹2,400/tCO2e |

> The correct commercial strategy: **always purchase deficit CCCs** during the trading window. The purchase cost (₹600–₹1,200/tCO2e) is always less than the penalty (₹1,200–₹2,400/tCO2e).

## 14. What Scope 2 Means — Electricity Emissions

Most industrial plants purchase electricity from the grid. The CO2 emitted at the power station to generate that electricity is attributed to the consumer as Scope 2 emissions.

**Formula:**
```
Scope 2 (tCO2e) = Electricity Purchased (MWh) × Grid Emission Factor (tCO2e/MWh)
```

**CEA Grid Emission Factors (FY 2022-23, latest published):**

| Region | Emission Factor |
|---|---|
| Northern Grid | 0.7047 tCO2e/MWh |
| Western Grid | 0.6843 tCO2e/MWh |
| Southern Grid | 0.5678 tCO2e/MWh |
| Eastern Grid | 0.9428 tCO2e/MWh |
| North-Eastern Grid | 0.4665 tCO2e/MWh |
| National Average | 0.7168 tCO2e/MWh |

> Source: Central Electricity Authority, CO2 Baseline Database for the Indian Power Sector

**Example:** A plant in Maharashtra (Western Grid) consuming 10,00,000 MWh/year:
```
Scope 2 = 10,00,000 MWh × 0.6843 tCO2e/MWh = 6,84,300 tCO2e
```

## 17. Limitations and Disclaimer

1. **Plant-specific baselines:** This tool cannot provide your exact BEE-notified baseline or target GEI — these are individual to each plant and published in the official gazette schedule. You must obtain your values from the gazette or your BEE notification letter.

2. **CCC price estimates:** The ₹600–₹1,200/tCO2e range is from analyst estimates only. CERC has not published an official floor/ceiling price band. Actual market prices will be set by supply/demand on the trading exchanges.

3. **Scope 2 electricity factors:** CEA emission factors change every year. Use the most current CEA CO2 Baseline Database (FY 2022-23 is the latest as of May 2026).

4. **ACVA verification is mandatory:** BEE will not accept Form A without a verification statement from a BEE-accredited Carbon Verification Agency (ACVA). The tool's output is for internal planning — it does not replace ACVA verification.

5. **Sub-sector nuances:** Aluminium smelters have very wide GEI variation (12.5–19.3 tCO2e/tonne). The petroleum refinery unit is MBBLS (million barrels of crude throughput) — not tonnes — which is unusual compared to all other sectors.

6. **Scope 3 not included:** CCTS covers only Scope 1 + Scope 2. Supply chain emissions (Scope 3) are not part of the GEI calculation.

7. **Fertiliser obligation threshold:** BEE uses a facility-named approach for fertiliser (21 entities), not a simple TOE threshold. Check whether your plant is named in the gazette.

8. **Phase 2 implementation:** Final notification for Phase 2 sectors was Jan 13–16, 2026, meaning these entities had less lead time than Phase 1 entities. BEE has acknowledged this and the MRV guidance for Phase 2 was issued concurrently.
